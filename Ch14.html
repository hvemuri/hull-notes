Ch 14 Wiener Processes and Ito’s Lemma

A variable whose value changes over time in an uncertain way follows a stochastic process.

  • stochastic processes can be discrete time or continuous time
  • stochastic processes can be discrete variable or continuous variable

The Markov Property

A Markov process is a type of stochastic process where only the current value of a variable is relevant for predicting the future. The history of the variable and the way the present state has emerged are irrelevant.

  • stock prices assumed to follow a Markov process
  • predictions for future are uncertain, must be expressed in terms of probability distributions
  • Markov property implies the probability distribution of the price at any particular future time is not dependent on the particular path followed by the price in the past

Markov property of stock prices is consistent with the weak form of market efficiency.

  • present price of a stock includes all information contained in a record of past prices
  • if weak form efficiency were not true, technical analysts could make above-average returns
    – there is little evidence that they can do this

Competition in the marketplace ensures weak-form efficiency and the Markov property hold.

  • many investors watching the market closely
  • patterns causing a stock price to rise would lead to high demand for the stock
  • this leads to the stock price rising, eliminating the observed effect

Continuous-Time Stochastic Processes

Consider a variable that follows a Markov stochastic process.

Current value is 10. The change in its value during a year is ϕ(0,1)\phi(0, 1)ϕ(0,1).

ϕ(m,v)\phi(m, v)ϕ(m,v) denotes a probability that is normally distributed with mean mmm and variance vvv.

What is the probability distribution of the change in the value of the variable during 2 years?

The change in 2 years is the sum of two normal distributions, each of which has a mean of zero and a variance of 1. Because the variable is Markov, the two probability distributions are independent.

If ϕ1\phi_1ϕ1​ and ϕ2\phi_2ϕ2​ are independent normal distributions, then the sum is given by

ϕ1(m1,v1)+ϕ2(m2,v2)=ϕ(m1+m2,v1+v2)\phi_1(m_1, v_1) + \phi_2(m_2, v_2) = \phi(m_1 + m_2, v_1 + v_2)ϕ1​(m1​,v1​)+ϕ2​(m2​,v2​)=ϕ(m1​+m2​,v1​+v2​)

ϕ(0,1)+ϕ(0,1)=ϕ(0,2)\phi(0, 1) + \phi(0, 1) = \phi(0, 2)ϕ(0,1)+ϕ(0,1)=ϕ(0,2)

So the mean of the change during 2 years in the variable is 0 and the variance of the change is 2.
The standard deviation of the change is 2\sqrt{2}2​.

What is the change in the variable during 6 months?

The variance of the change during 1 year equals the variance of the change during the first 6 months plus the variance of the change in the second 6 months. Assume these are the same.
So the variance of the change during a 6-month period is 0.5. Then the standard deviation of the change is 0.5\sqrt{0.5}0.5​. The probability distribution is ϕ(0,0.5)\phi(0, 0.5)ϕ(0,0.5).

A similar argument shows the probability distribution for the change in the value of the variable during 3 months is ϕ(0,0.25)\phi(0, 0.25)ϕ(0,0.25).

Generally, the change during the any time period of length TTT is ϕ(0,T)\phi(0, T)ϕ(0,T).
The change during a very short time period of length Δt\Delta tΔt is ϕ(0,Δt)\phi(0, \Delta t)ϕ(0,Δt).

Wiener Process

The process followed by the variable above is a Wiener process. It is a type of Markov stochastic process with a mean change of zero and a variance rate of 1.0 per year. It is closely related to Brownian motion.

A variable zzz follows a Wiener process if it has the following two properties:

Property 1. \text{Property 1. }Property 1.  The change Δz\Delta zΔz during a small period of time Δt\Delta tΔt is Δz=ϵΔt\Delta z = \epsilon \sqrt{\Delta t}Δz=ϵΔt​

Property 2. \text{Property 2. }Property 2.  The values of Δz\Delta zΔz for any two different intervals of time Δt\Delta tΔt are independent.

It follows from the first property that Δz\Delta zΔz itself has a normal distribution with

mean of Δz=0\text{mean of } \Delta z = 0mean of Δz=0

standard deviation of Δz=Δt\text{standard deviation of } \Delta z = \sqrt{\Delta t}standard deviation of Δz=Δt​

variance of Δz=Δt\text{variance of } \Delta z = \Delta tvariance of Δz=Δt

The second property implies that zzz follows a Markov process.

Consider the change in the value of zzz during a long period of time TTT, z(T)−z(0)z(T) - z(0)z(T)−z(0).

Note that the standard deviation of the movement in zzz in time Δt\Delta tΔt equals Δt\sqrt{\Delta t}Δt​.
As Δt→0\Delta t \rightarrow 0Δt→0, Δt>Δt\sqrt{\Delta t} > \Delta tΔt​>Δt. This results in the two properties below.

Two properties of Wiener processes:

  1. The expected length of the path followed by zzz in any time interval is infinite.
  2. The expected number of times zzz equals any particular value in any time interval is infinite.

This is because zzz has nonzero probability of equaling any value vvv in the time interval.
It it equals vvv in time ttt, the expected number of times it equals vvv in the vicinity of ttt is infinite.

Generalized Wiener Process

drift rate - the mean change per unit time for a stochastic process
variance rate - the variance per unit time for a stochastic process

The Wiener process considered so far has drift rate zero and variance rate 1.

  • drift rate zero means expected value of zzz at any future time equals its current value
  • variance rate 1 means the variance of the change in zzz in time interval of length TTT equals TTT

A generalized Wiener process for a variable xxx can be defined in terms of dzdzdz as

dx=a dt+b dzdx = a\,dt + b \,dzdx=adt+bdz

where aaa and bbb are constants.

The a dta\,dtadt terms implies that xxx has an expected drift rate of aaa per unit of time.
Without the second term, the equation is dx=a dtdx = a\,dtdx=adt, which implies dxdt=a\frac{dx}{dt} = adtdx​=a.

Integrate with respect to time x=x0+atx = x_0 + atx=x0​+at

where x0x_0x0​ is the value of xxx at time 0. In period of length TTT, variable xxx increases by amount aTaTaT.

The b dzb\,dzbdz term adds noise or variability to the path followed by xxx. The amount of this noise or variability is bbb times a Wiener process. A Wiener process has variance rate of 1, so bbb times a Wiener process has a variance rate per unit time of b2b^2b2.

In a small time interval Δt\Delta tΔt, the change in the value of xxx is given by Δx=aΔt+bϵΔt\Delta x = a \Delta t + b \epsilon \sqrt{\Delta t}Δx=aΔt+bϵΔt​ where ϵ∼ϕ(0,1)\epsilon \sim \phi(0, 1)ϵ∼ϕ(0,1).

This means Δx\Delta xΔx has a normal distribution with

mean of Δx=aΔt\text{mean of } \Delta x = a \Delta tmean of Δx=aΔt

standard deviation of Δx=bΔt\text{standard deviation of } \Delta x = b\sqrt{\Delta t}standard deviation of Δx=bΔt​

variance of Δx=b2Δt\text{variance of } \Delta x = b^2 \Delta tvariance of Δx=b2Δt

Similar arguments can show that the change in the value of xxx in any time interval TTT is normally distributed with

mean of x=aT\text{mean of } x = aTmean of x=aT

standard deviation of x=bT\text{standard deviation of } x = b\sqrt{T}standard deviation of x=bT​

So the generalized Wiener process given above has expected drift rate aaa and variance rate b2b^2b2.

Ito Process

An Ito process is a generalized Wiener process in which the parameters aaa and bbb are functions of the value of the underlying variable xxx and time ttt.

dx=a(x,t) dt+b(x,t) dzdx = a(x, t)\,dt + b(x, t)\,dzdx=a(x,t)dt+b(x,t)dz

Both the expected drift rate and variance rate are liable to change over time.

In a small interval between ttt and t+Δtt + \Delta tt+Δt, the variable changes from xxx to x+Δxx + \Delta xx+Δx, where

Δx=a(x,t)Δt+b(x,t)ϵΔt\Delta x = a(x, t) \Delta t + b(x, t) \epsilon \sqrt{\Delta t}Δx=a(x,t)Δt+b(x,t)ϵΔt​

This assumes the drift and variance rate of xxx do not change during the interval ttt to t+Δtt + \Delta tt+Δt.

Note that the Ito process as written above is a Markov process because the change in xxx and time ttt depends only on the value of xxx at time ttt and not on its history. A non-Markov Ito process can be defined by letting aaa and bbb depend on the values of xxx prior to time ttt.

The Process for a Stock Price

Stock price does not follow a generalized Wiener process (constant expected drift rate and constant variance rate) because the expected return required by investors is independent of the stock’s price.

The assumption of constant expected drift rate must be replaced by the assumption that the expected return (expected drift divided by stock price) is constant.

Let SSS be the stock price at time ttt. Then the expected drift rate is μS\mu SμS for some constant μ\muμ.
This means in a short interval of time Δt\Delta tΔt, the expected increase in SSS is μSΔt\mu S \Delta tμSΔt.
The a parameter μ\muμ is the expected rate of return on the stock.

If the coefficient of dzdzdz is zero (no uncertainty), then the model implies that ΔS=μSΔt\Delta S = \mu S \Delta tΔS=μSΔt

In the limit Δ→0\Delta \rightarrow 0Δ→0 ds=μS dtds = \mu S \, dtds=μSdt

dSS=μ dt\frac{dS}{S} = \mu \, dtSdS​=μdt

Integrate between time 000 and time TTT

ln(ST)−ln(S0)=ln(STS0)=μTln(S_T) - ln(S_0) = ln\left( \frac{S_T}{S_0} \right) = \mu Tln(ST​)−ln(S0​)=ln(S0​ST​​)=μT

ST=S0eμTS_T = S_0 e^{\mu T}ST​=S0​eμT

where S0S_0S0​ and STS_TST​ are the stock price at time 000 and time TTT.

This shows that when there is no uncertainty, the stock price grows at a continuously compounded rate of μ\muμ per unit of time.

In practice, there is uncertainty. Assume the variability of the return in a short period of time Δt\Delta tΔt is the same regardless of the stock price. This suggests that the standard deviation of the change in a short period of time Δt\Delta tΔt should be proportional to the stock price.

dS=μS dt+σS dzdS = \mu S \, dt + \sigma S \, dzdS=μSdt+σSdz

dSS=μ dt+σ dz\frac{dS}{S} = \mu \, dt + \sigma \, dzSdS​=μdt+σdz

This is a widely used model of stock price behavior.

  • μ\muμ is the stock’s expected rate of return
  • σ\sigmaσ is the volatility of the stock price
  • σ2\sigma^2σ2 is the variance rate

In a risk-neutral world, μ\muμ equals the risk-free rate rrr.

The Parameters

The parameter μ\muμ is the annualized expected return earned in a short period of time.

  • most investors require higher expected returns to induce them to take higher risks
  • so μ\muμ should depend on the risk of the return from the stock
  • it should also depend on the level of interest rates in the economy
    – higher interest rates means higher the expected return required on a given stock

However, the determinants of μ\muμ are not a concern because the value of a derivative dependent on a stock is in general independent of μ\muμ.

On the other hand, the parameter σ\sigmaσ (stock price volatility) is important to the determination of the value of many derivatives.

The standard deviation of the proportional change in the stock price in Δt\Delta tΔt is σΔt\sigma \sqrt{\Delta t}σΔt​.
The standard deviation of the proportional change over a long period TTT is roughly σT\sigma \sqrt{T}σT​.

This means volatility can be approximated as the standard deviation of the change in the stock price in 1 year. The volatility of the stock price is actually exactly equal to the standard deviation of the continuously compounded return provided by the stock in 1 year.

Correlated Processes

Now extend the analysis to two or more variables following correlated stochastic processes.

Suppose the processes followed by two variables x1x_1x1​ and x2x_2x2​ are

dx1=a1 dt+b1 dz1      and     dx2=a2 dt+b2 dz2dx_1 = a_1\,dt + b_1\,dz_1 \text{ \,\,\,\,\,and\,\,\,\,\, } dx_2 = a_2\,dt + b_2\,dz_2dx1​=a1​dt+b1​dz1​ anddx2​=a2​dt+b2​dz2​

where dz1dz_1dz1​ and dz2dz_2dz2​ are Wiener processes.

Δx1=a1Δt+b1ϵ1Δt      and     Δx2=a2Δt+b2ϵ2Δt\Delta x_1 = a_1 \Delta t + b_1 \epsilon_1 \sqrt{\Delta t} \text{ \,\,\,\,\,and\,\,\,\,\, } \Delta x_2 = a_2 \Delta t + b_2 \epsilon_2 \sqrt{\Delta t}Δx1​=a1​Δt+b1​ϵ1​Δt​ andΔx2​=a2​Δt+b2​ϵ2​Δt​

where ϵ1\epsilon_1ϵ1​ and ϵ2\epsilon_2ϵ2​ are samples from a standard normal distribution ϕ(0,1)\phi(0, 1)ϕ(0,1).

If the correlation ρ(x1,x2)=0\rho(x_1, x_2) = 0ρ(x1​,x2​)=0, then the random samples ϵ1\epsilon_1ϵ1​ and ϵ2\epsilon_2ϵ2​ should be independent.

If the correlation ρ(x1,x2)≠0\rho(x_1, x_2) \neq 0ρ(x1​,x2​)​=0, then ϵ1\epsilon_1ϵ1​ and ϵ2\epsilon_2ϵ2​ should be sampled from a bivariate normal distribution. Each variable in the bivariate normal distribution has a standard normal distribution and the correlation between the variables if ρ\rhoρ. The Wiener processes dz1dz_1dz1​ and dz2dz_2dz2​ are referred to as having a correlation ρ\rhoρ.

To sample standard normal variables ϵ1\epsilon_1ϵ1​ and ϵ2\epsilon_2ϵ2​ with correlation ρ\rhoρ

ϵ1=u      and     ϵ2=ρu+1−ρ2v\epsilon_1 = u \text{ \,\,\,\,\,and\,\,\,\,\, } \epsilon_2 = \rho u + \sqrt{1 - \rho^2 v}ϵ1​=u andϵ2​=ρu+1−ρ2v​

where uuu and vvv are sampled as uncorrelated variables with standard normal distributions.

Note that the parameters a1a_1a1​, a2a_2a2​, b1b_1b1​, and b2b_2b2​ can be functions of x1x_1x1​, x2x_2x2​, and ttt.
In fact, a1a_1a1​ and b1b_1b1​ can be functions of x2x_2x2​ as well as x1x_1x1​ and ttt.
And, a2a_2a2​ and b2b_2b2​ can be functions of x1x_1x1​ as well as x2x_2x2​ and ttt.

These results can be generalized to more than two variables as well. For three variables following correlated stochastic processes, sample three different ϵ\epsilonϵ's from a trivariate normal distribution. For nnn correlated variables, there are nnn different ϵ\epsilonϵ's sampled from an appropriate multivariate normal distribution.

The Lognormal Property

Use Ito’s lemma to derive the process followed by ln⁡S\ln{S}lnS when SSS follows the process dS=μS dt+σS dzdS = \mu S \, dt + \sigma S \, dzdS=μSdt+σSdz

The model of stock price behavior developed here implies that a stock’s price at time TTT, given its price today, is lognormally distributed.
