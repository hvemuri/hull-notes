Ch 28 Martingales and Measures

The risk-neutral valuation principle states that a derivative can be valued by:

  1. calculating the expected payoff on the assumption that the expected return from the underlying asset equals the risk-free interest rate
  2. discounting the expected payoff at the risk-free interest rate

traditional risk-neutral world - assumes that all market prices of risk are zero

martingale - a zero-drift stochastic process
measure - the unit in which security prices are valued

Equivalent martingale measure result

  • if the price of a traded security is used as the unit of measurement then there is a market price of risk for which all security prices follow martingales

The Market Price of Risk

Consider the properties of derivatives dependent on the value of a single variable θ\thetaθ.

Assume the process followed by θ\thetaθ is

dθθ=m dt+s dz\frac{d\theta}{\theta} = m \, dt + s \, dzθdθ​=mdt+sdz

where dzdzdz is a Wiener process, mmm is the expected growth rate in θ\thetaθ, and sss is the volatility of θ\thetaθ.

Let f1f_1f1​ and f2f_2f2​ be prices of two derivatives dependent only on θ\thetaθ and ttt.
Assume that during the time period under consideration the two derivatives provide no income.

Suppose the processes followed by f1f_1f1​ and f2f_2f2​ are

df1f1=μ1 dt+σ1 dz\frac{df_1}{f_1} = \mu_1 \, dt + \sigma_1 \, dzf1​df1​​=μ1​dt+σ1​dz

df2f2=μ2 dt+σ2 dz\frac{df_2}{f_2} = \mu_2 \, dt + \sigma_2 \, dzf2​df2​​=μ2​dt+σ2​dz

where μ1\mu_1μ1​, μ2\mu_2μ2​, σ1\sigma_1σ1​, and σ2\sigma_2σ2​ are functions of θ\thetaθ and ttt. The dzdzdz term is the same in all the equations above because it is the only source of uncertainty in the prices of f1f_1f1​ and f2f_2f2​.

The discrete versions of these processes are

Δf1=μ1f1Δt+σ1f1Δz\Delta f_1 = \mu_1 f_1 \Delta t + \sigma_1 f_1 \Delta zΔf1​=μ1​f1​Δt+σ1​f1​Δz

Δf2=μ2f2Δt+σ2f2Δz\Delta f_2 = \mu_2 f_2 \Delta t + \sigma_2 f_2 \Delta zΔf2​=μ2​f2​Δt+σ2​f2​Δz

Eliminate the Δz\Delta zΔz by forming an instantaneously riskless portfolio consisting of σ2f2\sigma_2f_2σ2​f2​ of the first derivative and −σ1f1-\sigma_1f_1−σ1​f1​ of the second derivative.

If Π\PiΠ is the value of the portfolio then

Π=(σ2f2)f1−(σ1f1)f2\Pi = (\sigma_2f_2)f_1 - (\sigma_1f_1)f_2Π=(σ2​f2​)f1​−(σ1​f1​)f2​

ΔΠ=σ2f2Δf1−σ1f1Δf2\Delta \Pi = \sigma_2f_2\Delta f_1 - \sigma_1f_1\Delta f_2ΔΠ=σ2​f2​Δf1​−σ1​f1​Δf2​

Substituting Δf1\Delta f_1Δf1​ and Δf2\Delta f_2Δf2​ from above gives

ΔΠ=(μ1σ2f1f2−μ2σ1f1f2)Δt\Delta \Pi = (\mu_1\sigma_2f_1f_2 - \mu_2\sigma_1f_1f_2)\Delta tΔΠ=(μ1​σ2​f1​f2​−μ2​σ1​f1​f2​)Δt

The portfolio is instantaneously riskless so it must earn the risk-free rate

ΔΠ=rΠΔt\Delta \Pi = r \Pi \Delta tΔΠ=rΠΔt

μ1σ2f1f2−μ2σ1f1f2=r(σ2f2f1−σ1f1f2)\mu_1\sigma_2f_1f_2 - \mu_2\sigma_1f_1f_2 = r(\sigma_2f_2f_1 - \sigma_1f_1f_2)μ1​σ2​f1​f2​−μ2​σ1​f1​f2​=r(σ2​f2​f1​−σ1​f1​f2​)

(f1f2)(μ1σ2−μ2σ1)=(r)(f1f2)(σ2−σ1)(f_1f_2)(\mu_1\sigma_2 - \mu_2\sigma_1) = (r)(f_1f_2)(\sigma_2-\sigma_1)(f1​f2​)(μ1​σ2​−μ2​σ1​)=(r)(f1​f2​)(σ2​−σ1​)

μ1σ2−μ2σ1=rσ2−rσ1\mu_1\sigma_2 - \mu_2\sigma_1 = r\sigma_2-r\sigma_1μ1​σ2​−μ2​σ1​=rσ2​−rσ1​

μ1−rσ1=μ2−rσ2=λ\frac{\mu_1-r}{\sigma_1} = \frac{\mu_2-r}{\sigma_2} = \lambdaσ1​μ1​−r​=σ2​μ2​−r​=λ

This shows that if fff is the price of a derivative dependent only on θ\thetaθ and ttt with

dff=μ dt+σ dz\frac{df}{f} = \mu \, dt + \sigma \, dzfdf​=μdt+σdz

then

μ−rσ=λ\frac{\mu - r}{\sigma} = \lambdaσμ−r​=λ

The parameter λ\lambdaλ is the market price of risk of θ\thetaθ (or Sharpe ratio).

The variable σ\sigmaσ is the quantity of θ\thetaθ-risk present in fff.
The absolute value ∣σ∣| \sigma |∣σ∣ is the volatility of fff.

Alternative Worlds

The process followed by derivative price fff is

df=μf dt+σf dzdf = \mu f \, dt + \sigma f \, dzdf=μfdt+σfdz

The value of μ\muμ depends on the risk preferences of investors.

In a world where market price of risk is zero, λ=0\lambda = 0λ=0, so μ=r\mu = rμ=r.

Then the process followed by fff is

df=rf dt+σf dzdf = r f \, dt + \sigma f \, dzdf=rfdt+σfdz

This is the traditional risk-neutral world.

Other assumptions about the market price of risk λ\lambdaλ enable other worlds that are internally consistent to be defined.

The market price of risk of a variable determines the growth rates of all securities dependent on the variable.

Girsanov’s Theorem

  • moving from one market price of risk to another changes the expected growth rates of security prices but volatilities remain the same

Choosing a particular market price of risk is referred to as defining the probability measure.

Some value of the market price of risk corresponds to the “real world”.

Several State Variables

Suppose that nnn variables θ1,θ2,…,θn\theta_1, \theta_2, \dots, \theta_nθ1​,θ2​,…,θn​ follow stochastic processes of the form

dθiθi=mi dt+si dzi\frac{d\theta_i}{\theta_i} = m_i \, dt + s_i \, dz_iθi​dθi​​=mi​dt+si​dzi​

where the dzidz_idzi​ are Wiener processes, and mim_imi​ and sis_isi​ are the expected growth rates and volatilities and may be functions of the θi\theta_iθi​ and time.

The process for the price fff of a security that is dependent on the θi\theta_iθi​ has nnn stochastic components and can be written

dff=μ dt+∑i=1nσi dzi\frac{df}{f} = \mu \, dt + \sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i \, dz_ifdf​=μdt+i=1∑n​σi​dzi​

where μ\muμ is the expected return from the security and σi dzi\sigma_i \, dz_iσi​dzi​ is the components of the risk of this return attributable to θi\theta_iθi​. Both μ\muμ and the σi\sigma_iσi​ are potentially dependent on the θi\theta_iθi​ and time.

It can be shown that

μ−r=∑i=1nλiσi\mu-r = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i \sigma_iμ−r=i=1∑n​λi​σi​

where λi\lambda_iλi​ is the market price of risk for θi\theta_iθi​.

The λiσi\lambda_i \sigma_iλi​σi​ terms measures the extent that the excess return required by investors on a security is affected by the dependence of the security on θi\theta_iθi​.

  • if λiσi=0\lambda_i \sigma_i = 0λi​σi​=0 then there is no effect
  • if λiσi>0\lambda_i \sigma_i > 0λi​σi​>0 then investors require a higher return to compensate for risk arising from θi\theta_iθi​
  • if λiσi<0\lambda_i \sigma_i < 0λi​σi​<0 then investors require a lower return due to dependence of the security on θi\theta_iθi​

This last scenario occurs when the variable has the effect of reducing rather than increasing the risks in the portfolio of a typical investor.

The capital asset pricing model (CAPM) argues that an investor requires excess returns to compensate for any risk that is correlated to the risk in the return from the stock market, but requires no excess return for other risks.

  • systematic risk - risks that are correlated with the return from the stock market
  • nonsystematic risk - other risks

If CAPM is true than λi\lambda_iλi​ is proportional to the correlation between changes in θi\theta_iθi​ and the return from the market. When θi\theta_iθi​ is uncorrelated with the return from the market then λi=0\lambda_i = 0λi​=0.

Martingales

martingale - a zero-drift stochastic process

A variable θ\thetaθ follows a martingale if its process has the form

dθ=σ dzd\theta = \sigma \, dzdθ=σdz

where dzdzdz is a Wiener process. The variable σ\sigmaσ may itself be stochastic.

A martingale has the property that its expected value at any future time is equal to its value today.

E(θt)=θ0E(\theta_t) = \theta_0E(θt​)=θ0​

Why?

Note that over a very small time interval the change in θ\thetaθ is normally distributed with zero mean.
So the expected change in θ\thetaθ over any very small time interval is zero.
The change in θ\thetaθ between time 000 and time TTT is the sum of its changes over many small time intervals. It follows that the expected change in θ\thetaθ between time 000 and time TTT must also be zero.

The Equivalent Martingale Measure Result

Suppose fff and ggg are the prices of traded securities dependent on a single source of uncertainty.
Assume the securities provide no income during the time period under consideration.

Define ϕ=f/g\phi = f/gϕ=f/g, the relative price of fff with respect to ggg.
It can be thought of as measuring the price of fff in units of ggg rather than dollars.
The security price ggg is the numeraire.

The equivalent martingale measure result shows that when there are no arbitrage opportunities, ϕ\phiϕ is a martingale for some choice of the market price of risk. Also, for a given numeraire security ggg, the same choice of the market price of risk makes ϕ\phiϕ a martingale for all securities fff. This choice of the market price of risk is the volatility of ggg.

In other words, when the market price of risk is set equal to the volatility of ggg, the ratio f/gf/gf/g is a martingale for all security prices fff.

A world where the market price of risk is the volatility σg\sigma_gσg​ of ggg is forward risk neutral with respect to ggg.

Because f/gf/gf/g is a martingale in a world that is forward risk neutral with respect to ggg, it follows, from the Martingale property given above, that

f0g0=Eg(fTgT)\frac{f_0}{g_0} = E_g\left(\frac{f_T}{g_T}\right)g0​f0​​=Eg​(gT​fT​​)

f0=g0Eg(fTgT)f_0 = g_0E_g\left(\frac{f_T}{g_T}\right)f0​=g0​Eg​(gT​fT​​)

where EgE_gEg​ is the expected value in a world that is forward risk neutral with respect to ggg.

Alternative Choices for the Numeraire

Money Market Account as the Numeraire

The dollar money market account is a security that is worth $1 at time zero and earns the instantaneous risk-free rate rrr at any given time. The variable rrr may be stochastic.

Set ggg equal to the money market account so it grows at rate rrr so that

dg=rg dtdg = rg \, dtdg=rgdt

The drift of ggg is stochastic but its volatility is zero. It follows that f/gf/gf/g is a martingale in a world where the market price of risk is zero.

From before

f0=g0E^(fTgT)f_0 = g_0 \hat{E}\left(\frac{f_T}{g_T}\right)f0​=g0​E^(gT​fT​​)

where E^\hat{E}E^ is expectation in the traditional risk-neutral world.

In this case g0=1g_0 = 1g0​=1 and

gT=e∫0Tr dtg_T = e^{\int_0^T r\,dt}gT​=e∫0T​rdt

so this gives

f0=E^(e−rˉTfT)f_0 = \hat{E}(e^{-\bar{r}T}f_T)f0​=E^(e−rˉTfT​)

where rˉ\bar{r}rˉ is the average value of rrr between time 000 and time TTT.

When the short-term interest rate rrr is assumed to be constant, this becomes

f0=e−rTE^(fT)f_0 = e^{-rT}\hat{E}(f_T)f0​=e−rTE^(fT​)

which is consistent with risk-neutral valuation.

Zero-Coupon Bond Price as the Numeraire

Let P(t,T)P(t, T)P(t,T) be price at time ttt of a risk-free zero-coupon bond that pays off $1 at time TTT.

Suppose g=P(t,T)g = P(t, T)g=P(t,T).

Let ETE_TET​ be expectations in a world that is forward risk neutral with respect to P(t,T)P(t, T)P(t,T).

gT=P(T,T)=1g_T = P(T, T) = 1gT​=P(T,T)=1

Note that the discounting is outside the expectations operator rather than inside, as it is with the money market account as numeraire. This simplifies things for a security that provides payoff solely at time TTT.

Consider any variable θ\thetaθ that is not an interest rate. A forward contract on θ\thetaθ with maturity TTT is defined as a contract that pays off θT−K\theta_T - KθT​−K at time TTT. Let fff be the value of this contract.

fT=θT−Kf_T = \theta_T - KfT​=θT​−K

The forward price FFF of θ\thetaθ is the value of KKK for which f0=0f_0 = 0f0​=0.

P(0,T)[ET(θT)−F]=0P(0, T)[E_T(\theta_T) - F] = 0P(0,T)[ET​(θT​)−F]=0

Recall that the futures price of a variable is the expected future spot price in the traditional risk-neutral world.

Black’s Model Revisited

Recall that Black’s model is a tool for pricing European options in terms of forward or futures price of the underlying asset when interest rates are constant. The constant interest rate assumption can be relaxed to show that Black’s model can be used to price European options in terms of the forward price of the underlying asset when interest rate are stochastic.

Consider a European call option on an asset with strike price KKK that lasts until time TTT.

c=P(0,T)ET(max⁡(ST−K,0)]c = P(0, T)E_T(\max(S_T - K, 0)]c=P(0,T)ET​(max(ST​−K,0)]

Define F0F_0F0​ and FTF_TFT​ as forward price of the asset at time 000 and time TTT for a contract maturing at time TTT.

Note that ST=FTS_T = F_TST​=FT​ which means

c=P(0,T)ET[max⁡(FT−K,0)]c = P(0, T)E_T[\max(F_T - K, 0)]c=P(0,T)ET​[max(FT​−K,0)]

Assume FTF_TFT​ is lognormal with standard deviation of ln⁡(FT)\ln(F_T)ln(FT​) equal to σFT\sigma_F\sqrt{T}σF​T​.
This could be because the forward price follows a stochastic process with volatility σF\sigma_FσF​.
