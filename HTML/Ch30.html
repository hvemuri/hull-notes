Ch 30 Convexity, Timing, and Quanto Adjustments

Two-step procedure for valuing a European-style derivative:

  1. Calculate expected payoff by assuming the expected value of each underlying variable equals its forward value
  2. Discount expected payoff at the risk-free rate applicable for the time period between valuation date and payoff date

For nonstandard interest rate derivatives, this procedure needs to be adjusted.

Three types of adjustments:

  • convexity adjustments
  • timing adjustments
  • quanto adjustments

Convexity Adjustments

Consider an instrument that provides a payoff dependent on a bond yield observed at the time of the payoff.

The forward value of a variable SSS is usually calculated with reference to a forward contract that pays off ST−KS_T - KST​−K at time TTT. The value of KKK causes the contract to have zero value.

Forward interest rates vs forward yields

  • forward interest rate is the rate implied by a forward zero-coupon bond
  • forward bond yield is the yield implied by the forward bond price

Suppose that BTB_TBT​ is the bond price at time TTT and let yTy_TyT​ be its yield.
The relationship between BTB_TBT​ and yTy_TyT​ is

By definition of forward bond yield

The function GGG is nonlinear. This means that when the expected future bond price equals the forward bond price (a world that is forward risk neutral with respect to a zero-coupon bond maturing at time TTT), the expected future bond yield does not equal the forward bond yield.

In this relationship between bond prices and bond yields, the bond prices are equally spaced but the corresponding bond yields are not. If the forward bond yield is B2B_2B2​ then the forward bond yield is y2y_2y2​. The expected bond yield is the average of y1y_1y1​, y2y_2y2​, and y3y_3y3​. This is greater than y2y_2y2​.

The expected bond price equals the forward price in the world being considered.

Quantos

A quanto or cross-currency derivative is an instrument where two currencies are involved.

The payoff is defined in terms of a variable that is measured in one of the currency and the payoff is made in the other currency.

Example: CME futures contract on the Nikkei

  • market variable underlying the contract is the Nikkei 225 index (measured in yen)
  • the contract is settled in US dollars

Consider a quanto that provides a payoff in currency XXX at time TTT.
Assume the payoff depends on value VVV of a variable that is observed in currency YYY at time TTT.

  • Let PX(t,T)P_X(t, T)PX​(t,T) be value at time ttt in currency XXX of a zero-coupon bond paying off 1 unit of currency XXX at time TTT
  • Let PY(t,T)P_Y(t, T)PY​(t,T) be value at time ttt in currency YYY of a zero-coupon bond paying off 1 unit of currency YYY at time TTT
  • Let VTV_TVT​ be value of VVV at time TTT
  • Let EX(VT)E_X(V_T)EX​(VT​) be expected value of VTV_TVT​ in a world that is forward risk neutral with respect to PX(t,T)P_X(t, T)PX​(t,T)
  • Let EY(VT)E_Y(V_T)EY​(VT​) be expected value of VTV_TVT​ in a world that is forward risk neutral with respect to PY(t,T)P_Y(t, T)PY​(t,T)

Using Traditional Risk-Neutral Measures

The forward risk-neutral measure works well when payoffs occur at only one time.
Other situations should use the traditional risk-neutral measure.

Suppose the process followed by a variable VVV in the traditional currency-YYY risk-neutral world is known and the goal is to estimate its process in the traditional currency-XXX risk-neutral world.

  • Let SSS be spot exchange rate (units of YYY per unit of XXX)
  • Let σS\sigma_SσS​ be volatility of SSS
  • Let σV\sigma_VσV​ be volatility of VVV
  • Let ρ\rhoρ be instantaneous correlation between SSS and VVV

Siegel’s Paradox

Consider two currencies, XXX and YYY. Suppose the interest rates in each are constant, rXr_XrX​ and rYr_YrY​.
Let SSS be number of units of currency YYY per unit of currency XXX.

Recall that a currency is an asset that provides a yield at the foreign risk-free rate.
So the traditional risk-neutral process for SSS is

dS=(rY−rX)S dt+σSS dzdS = (r_Y - r_X)S\,dt + \sigma_SS\,dzdS=(rY​−rX​)Sdt+σS​Sdz

From Ito’s lemma this implies the process for 1/S1/S1/S is

d(1/S)=(rX−rY+σS2)(1/S) dt−σ(1/S) dzd(1/S) = (r_X - r_Y + \sigma_S^2)(1/S)\,dt - \sigma(1/S)\,dzd(1/S)=(rX​−rY​+σS2​)(1/S)dt−σ(1/S)dz

This leads to the paradox.

Expected growth rate of SSS is rY−rXr_Y - r_XrY​−rX​ in a risk-neutral world.
Symmetry suggests that the expected growth rate of 1/S1/S1/S should be rX−rYr_X - r_YrX​−rY​ rather than rX−rY+σS2r_X - r_Y + \sigma_S^2rX​−rY​+σS2​.

Why?

The process given for SSS is the risk-neutral process for SSS in a world where the numeraire is the money market account in currency YYY.

The process for 1/S1/S1/S also assumes this numeraire.

However 1/S1/S1/S is the number of units of XXX per unit of YYY. To be symmetrical, the process for 1/S1/S1/S should be measured in a world where the numeraire is the money market account in currency XXX.

Applying a change in numeraire accordingly, the growth rate of a variable VVV increases by ρσVσS\rho\sigma_V\sigma_SρσV​σS​, as shown above. Since V=1/SV = 1/SV=1/S in this case, ρ=−1\rho = -1ρ=−1 and σV=σS\sigma_V = \sigma_SσV​=σS​. It follows that the change of numeraire causes growth rate of 1/S1/S1/S to increase by −σS2-\sigma_S^2−σS2​. This neutralizes the +σS2+\sigma_S^2+σS2​ in the process given above.

So the process for 1/S1/S1/S in a world where the numeraire is the money market account in currency XXX is

d(1/S)=(rX−rY)(1/S) dt−σS(1/S) dzd(1/S) = (r_X - r_Y)(1/S)\,dt - \sigma_S(1/S)\,dzd(1/S)=(rX​−rY​)(1/S)dt−σS​(1/S)dz

which is consistent with the process for SSS.

So the paradox is resolved.
